As we finalized the first phase of the consumer app and began refining designs for further testing, a key concern emerged: users didn't see the need to create accounts. To ensure decisions were data-driven, I conducted a mix of quantitative surveys and diary studies. Learn more about Phase I →

Recap.

Phase I uncovered six critical pain points: users found no added value in creating accounts; frustration with the "ping-pong" magic link log-in; missing vital hub instructions buried under the email CTA; struggling with an unfamiliar UI; chatbot support expectation; and desire for live tracking and notifications.

Pivotal question.

The findings raised a pivotal question: Do we need a native app for the beta version, or would a web app suffice? When I raised this concern with stakeholders, they unanimously agreed to gather quantified data to inform the decision.

Context.

Goals: Understand user behaviors and expectations for managing order pick-up and returns. Validate whether a native app provides sufficient value compared to a web app for beta functionalities.

Key research questions: What are the key pain points from placing an order to pick-up/return using lockers? How often do users interact with locker services, and through which platforms? What level of added value would justify downloading a native app? Do users feel account creation is necessary?

Methodology.

Quantitative Surveys: Distributed to 300 users familiar with locker services like DHL Lockers, MyFlexBox. Focused on frequency of locker service use, preferred platform (web vs. native app), expectations for features, and willingness to create accounts.

Diary Studies: 10 participants actively using lockers documented their journey over 2 weeks — their process from placing an order to pick-up/return, pain points, emotions, and friction points.

Key insights.

A total of 263 participants completed the survey, achieving an 87.7% response rate. Web apps were the preferred method for accessing parcel lockers over native apps.

72% — Younger and middle-aged users (18–44) are the primary audience for parcel lockers and frequent online shoppers.

35% — Parcel locker popularity growing in delivery preferences, scoring second to home delivery.

9% — Only a minority favored native apps, for better control and frequent shopping needs.

Survey data visualization — top challenges, locker providers, valued features

Diary studies highlight.

Common Journey: Most users simply scanned the QR code from the merchant email using DHL lockers with interactive displays. Web-app operated locker: One participant encountered a screenless locker for the first time, initially felt confused, but quickly adapted. Native-app: A frequent online shopper used the MyDHL app to manage delivery preferences.

Display screens are central to user experience. Web apps serve as a reliable backup. Native apps have niche appeal for frequent shoppers. Account creation resistance persists. Inconsistent locker interfaces reduce satisfaction.

Balancing Innovation and User Familiarity.

While research revealed most lockers use built-in screens, bace chose screenless lockers for cost efficiency and scalability, reduced hardware components and maintenance, and a focus on digital transformation with an app-first approach that ensures consistent experience across all lockers.

Recommendations.

1. Launch a Web App for the Beta — Web apps align with user preferences for low-commitment, one-time interactions typical of locker services. Developing a web app requires significantly fewer resources compared to a native app.

2. Enhance Instructions — Phase I insights revealed unclear locker instructions as a major pain point.

3. Focus on enhancing the user flow — Phase I findings revealed key pain points in the overall flow that could deter users.

4. Post-Beta Plan: Launching a Native App with Innovative Features — Introduce valuable features to incentivize users to download and actively use the app.

Impact.

Prioritizing a web app for the beta saved costs, reduced time-to-market risks, and enabled early user feedback collection.

€50K–€70K saved in estimated development costs. 3–5 fewer team members required (1–2 product designers and 2–3 developers). 2–3 months faster time to market for quicker testing and feedback.

The research insights also impacted marketing (content creation and brand positioning), strategy and business development (C-level insights, new opportunities), and investor pitches.

Reflection.

What went well: combining surveys and diary studies provided robust, actionable findings. Video recordings from diary studies captured real user frustrations. Early alignment with product, design, and engineering teams ensured research outcomes were actionable.

Challenges: ensuring participants consistently recorded diary entries required frequent follow-up. Convincing stakeholders initially favoring a native app of the web app's advantages required carefully presenting quantitative evidence.
